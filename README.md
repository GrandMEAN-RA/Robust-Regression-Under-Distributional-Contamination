
                   
# ABSTRACT

The Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) estimator is the standard approach for linear regression; however, its reliance on normality and homoscedasticity assumptions renders it highly susceptible to breakdown in the presence of outliers and heavy-tailed error distributions. This study employs the ADEMP and Tidy Simulation frameworks to conduct an extensive Monte-Carlo simulation (150,000 replications) investigating the behaviour of selected classical, robust, and Bayesian regression estimators for the Simple Linear Regression model. Simulations were performed across six sample sizes (n=10 –1000) and four error-generating environments (Standard, Contamination, Outlier, and Mixture models) to compare the performance of Ordinary Least Squares (OLS), Least Trimmed Squares (LTS), Theil’s pair-wise median, and Bayesian regression evaluated using bias, mean square error (MSE), Monte Carlo standard error (MCSE), relative mean square error (RMSE), and interval coverage probability. In addition, kernel density estimation was employed to examine the empirical behaviour of the simulated error distribution and to assess structural stability, tail behaviour, and convergence characteristics under different contamination mechanisms. Our results indicate that Theil’s method consistently exhibits the highest reliability across all conditions, though its O(n^2) computational complexity limits its application in very large datasets. The LTS method is identified as the optimal trade-off between computational efficiency and robustness for large-scale samples (n  ≥ 500). Conversely, we find that standard Bayesian regression, when implemented without robust heavy-tailed priors, remains as vulnerable to outliers as OLS. These findings provide clear empirical guidance for choosing robust statistical frameworks in practical applications, demonstrating that transitioning away from OLS is essential when data deviates from Gaussian assumptions. 
Keywords: Robust Statistics; Monte Carlo Simulation; Breakdown Point; Estimator Efficiency, Outlier Resistance, Heavy-Tailed Distributions, Simple Linear Regression.
